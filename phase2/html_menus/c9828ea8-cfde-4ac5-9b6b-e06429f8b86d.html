The Shapeshifting Pandan Leaf Has Its Moment in the Sun

By Samuel Anderson Photographer Fujio Emura
November 2, 2022

With all the recent upheaval to the New York food scene— curbside dining , rodent invasions, celebrity meltdowns that feel indicative of a broader class war—for a single ingredient to impact the conversation is no small feat. But over the past few years, the bright green pandan plant has done just that, cropping up in bold new forms in red-hot kitchens and bakeries all across the city.

Long cultivated across Southeast Asia, pandan will need no introduction for many. Its complex flavor—the product of a compound also found in white bread and jasmine rice—is ubiquitous in the region, imparting smooth, nutty, slightly herbal notes to everything from Malaysia’s national dish nasi lemak (a colorful breakfast centered on pandan-infused rice) to Singapore’s McFlurries .

Though often compared to vanilla, pandan tastes and behaves quite unlike anything else. Its emerald blades, which grow up to seven feet in the wild, can take any number of life paths once harvested: When reduced to a powder or extract , pandan is both a flavor agent and food coloring. Left intact, the leaves become a potent aromatic when cooked or steamed alongside grains or proteins. (Too tough to eat whole, the cooked leaves are typically removed prior to serving.) Pandan can alternately be chopped and blended into batters or milks, spread on toast as a custardy jam , or boiled in water for a super-simple tonic. Pandan desserts, meanwhile, are a category unto themselves. From traditional kuih (steamed jelly-like custards found across Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia) to handheld waffles , they span most every Southeast Asian cuisine, according to food blogger Jun.

The latter category—desserts—has fueled pandan crazes past and present. In the mid-20th century, according to Saveur , a widely circulated Better Homes and Gardens recipe spawned an international sensation: pandan chiffon cake. In present-day New York, that kelly-green sponge continues to make waves: At the Ha’s Đặc Biệt pop-up last winter, baker-in-residence Mina Park of Ninety Nine Cakes struck a chord with a riff on the pandan snack-cakes of her native Atlanta’s Vietnamese supermarkets. “It was the one cake I kept on the menu for the whole three-month pop-up,” she says. “To have that kind of staying power…I think it meant something.”

To Park, the meaning comes down to more than taste alone. “Pandan [is] a flavor I’m always thinking of—it’s always in the back of my mind,” she says. “My eyes were always drawn to that neon green hue…That’s where my nostalgia is.” For some, the gestalt of pandan goes even further back than childhood. “My absolute favorite aspect of cooking with pandan is that it allows me to commune with my auntie ancestors,” says Doris Hồ-Kane of Cobble Hill’s Bạn Bè , dubbed New York’s first Vietnamese-American bakery. “I become a part of our unique food lineage.”

Thanks to entrepreneurs like Park and Hồ-Kane, that lineage is finding new expression in the New York area, where the full spectrum of Southeast Asian cuisine has historically been underrepresented compared to the West Coast and parts of the South. At Bạn Bè, which opened a brick-and-mortar outpost in July following a string of über-successful pop-ups, pandan-infused confections like butter cookies and pandan-coconut frappes channel its founder’s Texas upbringing. “[My] favorite early memories of pandan are having it in fresh, warm soybean milk and in bánh bò hấp, a steamed ‘honeycomb’ rice cake,” Hồ-Kane says. “The green wash of color was so beautiful to me… It still is!”

Within the upswell of new restaurants specializing in regional Southeast Asian cuisines, pandan has become a recurring theme. Kuih Cafe , which became the city’s first Malaysian dessert shop when it opened on Eldridge Street in February of 2020, uses hand-pressed pandan extract for its nonpareil pastries (see: kuih seri muka, a blue-green sticky rice cake topped with pandan custard). “It’s a nostalgic feeling,” says Barnny Lim, business partner of chef and owner Veronica Gan. “The reason [Veronica] started baking kuih was that sometimes she’d feel homesick…We’d go to Malaysian restaurants or markets and find very few [dessert] options.” Despite a temporary closure following the cafe’s ill-timed launch, Gan’s handiwork continues to draw lines down the block.

Whether the object of nostalgia or a newfound novelty, pandan products are a proven crowd-pleaser. “There was definitely a pandan craze—I’d go to Lady Wong [bakery] and couldn’t get any of the pandan kuih because people were going crazy for it,” says Park, referring to the Singaporean-style patisserie opened by husband-and-wife team Mogan Anthony and Seleste Tan in February—the success of which has already led to a second location in the new street-food-inspired Urban Hawker food hall. For New York transplants like Park, the swift expansion of Southeast Asian offerings comes as a welcome corrective. “I think people are starting to understand the wider scope of Asian cooking and culture,” says the cake-maker. “I think it’s about Southeast Asian voices and perspectives being heard for the first time in the mainstream.”

For pastry chef Lauren Tran, pandan played a winning role in bridging those gaps. As a rookie chef at Gramercy Tavern, she won the restaurant’s prestigious pie contest with a pandan-lemongrass coconut cream pie—flavors she might not have chosen had she not moved to New York from Seattle in 2018. “Until I moved to New York, I hadn’t baked with [pandan] before, because it was so accessible [on the West Coast],” says Tran. “New York is so diverse but I realized it was actually pretty scarce when it comes to Southeast Asian desserts.” When it came to launching her own company, Bánh by Lauren , the need for more comprehensive culinary representation was again a north star. “That was the whole reason why I did this—because, during COVID, I was really craving Vietnamese desserts and I couldn't find them anywhere. I was like, ‘Okay, I’m going to do what I want to do with the flavors that I like,’” she says. Currently operating out of her Brooklyn kitchen and at periodic pop-ups, Tran plans to have a permanent space for homestyle treats, which include the ever-popular pandan chiffon cake and a jewel-toned bánh bò loaf. “I think a lot of people in New York are doing [what I did],” Tran adds. “They’re like, ‘I can showcase what is special to my culture that I don’t see here, and hopefully there are other people who miss it and want it too.’”

Indeed, bakers aren’t the only ones driving up pandan demand. At Zaab Zaab in Elmhurst, which specializes in Isan Thai food, chef Aniwat Khotsopa stuffs the charcoal-roasted catfish with pandan and lemongrass. For its “Tiger Killer” and “Knockout Tiger” cocktails, Thai-Japanese speakeasy 11 Tigers stocks pandan-flavored rum and pandan ya dong , a Thai version of moonshine. And at Madame Vo, pandan adds an extra colorful kick to the Vietnamese iced latte .

Nor is pandan pandemonium limited to professional kitchens. Joe Boo, co-founder of the e-commerce delivery platform Asian-Veggies.com and the son of a Queens produce wholesaler, launched his DTC service in 2020 to streamline a fragmented marketplace. Though he envisioned frozen pandan leaves as a “gateway” product—one that would drive awareness of the site by showing up in web searches—they proved strong sellers in themselves. “We know that pandan is a highly demanded product,” says Boo. “When we launched pandan on our site, we sold out three or four cases within a week, which is a lot. That’s like 200 individual orders.”

Each contains not only a cooking-and-baking power tool, but, as Kopitiam founder Kyo Pang suggests, the chance for a unique encounter as well. “My grandmother would say pandan is like a person, [in that] a person can be anything,” she says. “When I blend it or squeeze it or whatever, it’s like the leaf was asleep and now I’m waking it up.” Having opened Kopitiam in 2015, Pang maintains that soul connection through dishes like the pandan-wrapped chicken, which she still assembles herself. “The [recipe] is actually from my maternal grandmother,” she explains. “It’s hard to train people how to do the triangle. It’s very traditional, and very time-consuming, but it’s the original.”

By now a standard bearer in the City’s Malaysian food scene, Pang takes a familial pride in her pandan presentation, while seeing plenty of room for others’ interpretations. “Before Kopitiam, I don’t think anyone was talking about nasi lemak and kaya toast,” Pang says. “[But] I don’t want to be the only one [making it]. I’m always glad to see more… It feels like the culture isn’t dying.”
