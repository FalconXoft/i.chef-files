World-renowned chef and opera singer dines at Staten Island restaurant

  • Updated: Feb. 23, 2024, 4:35 p.m.
  • |Published: Feb. 17, 2024, 10:00 a.m.
By
  • Dr. Gracelyn Santos | gsantos@siadvance.com

STATEN ISLAND, N.Y. — The culinary gem of Stapleton, soul food restaurant Shaw-nae’s House, welcomed a very special guest recently, when Alexander Smalls, an acclaimed chef, author, and storyteller came to dine with restauranteur Shaw-nae Dixon herself, and Laurie Cumbo, the New York City’s Commissioner of Cultural Affairs.

A New York City icon, Smalls is in the middle of opening the city’s first African food hall, Alkebulan, planned for Harlem, which will feature 10 food stalls, music, and art from around the continent. The first African food hall opened in Dubai, with stalls that include Shoebox Bakery, Chicken Coop, and Sweet Ophelia’s, an Afro-Asian wok bar. (Shaw-nae is planning a trip to Dubai in June to visit Alkebulan.)

Recipient of a James Beard Award in 2019 for his cookbook “Between Harlem and Heaven,” Smalls also co-owned the esteemed restaurants The Cecil and Minton’s.

The Cecil, recognized as NYC’s inaugural Afro-Asian-American eatery, earned the prestigious title of “Best New Restaurant in America” by Esquire in 2014. Situated in historic Harlem, Minton’s is renowned as the birthplace of BeBop in the 1930s.

“By way of a unique blessing and God-sent favor, my sister, Laurie (Commissioner Cumbo), made an infinite connection between past, present, and future,” said Dixon, delighted beyond words to welcome an esteemed guest such as Smalls. “I served up a soul food dinner to the world-renowned soul food chef extraordinaire. Alex, Laurie and myself discovered a bond that can never be broken or recreated. It was, and will always, be what God ordained.”

DINING WITH THE LEGEND

So, what did the iconic Smalls — whose latest culinary book, “Meals, Music, and Muses: Recipes from My African American Kitchen” (2020), pays homage to his South Carolina roots and musical influences — have for dinner at Shaw-nae’s House on Van Duzer Street?

“Alex’s entree had a whole fried golden double fried crispy Cornish hen over jambalaya rice with side dishes of mac and cheese sweet potatoes, collard greens, and a helping of oxtails,” said Dixon. “He mentioned that the rice was his first desired bite because it reminded him of his roots in the south. When he went to indulge in the oxtails, he said he would put my oxtails up against known Lincoln Center Chef Kwame of the renowned Tatiana’s any day.

“He also enjoyed a shoe shoe platter with angel eggs, fried catfish nuggets, dry rub wings, fried colossal shrimp, and corn bread bites. He knew that there was sweet pickle relish in the angel eggs and pointed out that you should never have bread and butter or diced kosher dill pickles.”

Peach cobbler sweet potato pie and apple pie with vanilla ice cream were carefully prepared for dessert, after dinner paired with a bottle of red wine and a lively conversation about family, soul food, the “journey to growing in your own gifts,” and future partnerships.

“Alexander spoke about his journey through the arts as an opera vocalist and spoke briefly about his James Beard acknowledgement and how he desires to inspire chefs to improve their own gifts, create the design of their own journey, and be excited by our own achievements,” added Dixon. “He was was compassionate, funny, and pure during our visit. He said he had only been to Staten Island three times.

“He said he never had a reason to be here (on Staten Island), but Shaw-nae’s House gives him a new reason to visit more often. When Alex left, we kissed goodbye, and I received a text message of confirmation that the job was more than well done. He said he was eating the leftovers the next day and couldn’t wait to have more.”

SURREAL EXPERIENCE

A few days removed from the unforgettable visit of this legendary chef, Dixon is still coming down from her Culinary Cloud 9.

A chef, restaurateur, and opera singer — who won both a Grammy Award and Tony Award for the cast recording of George Gershwin’s “Porgy and Bess,” with the Houston Grand Opera — Smalls is commonly regarded as the grandfather of New York’s Black culture. He is also known for hosting some of the city’s best dinner parties.

“As I gaze the photos (of the evening) captured the by the talented photographer Ar Yum, I am in awe at what God is doing at this very moment,” said Dixon. “My restaurant was bustling with diverse support and good company. Everyone was at home eating dishes I created. Alex was in awe at what was happening on the borough of untapped power and potential and he, too, saw memories being made.

“We are no longer the forgotten borough, we are now the borough of moments to be remembered. Soul Food has a home on Staten Island. It is the safe space and sacred ground of a Sandy Ground descendant. It is also the place I will grow into an overflow so that it does reach many cities, states and countries.”

MORE ABOUT ALEXANDER SMALLS

Over the span of three decades, Smalls has traveled the globe, immersing himself in the culinary techniques and traditions of the African diaspora. Renowned as the former chef-owner of esteemed establishments like Café Beulah, Sweet Ophelia’s, and Shoebox Café, Smalls has garnered widespread recognition within the culinary sphere.

His accolades include gracing the kitchen of the James Beard House, earning a spot on Zagat’s esteemed list of “NYC Restaurant Power Players You Need to Know,” and receiving the esteemed Legacy Award in 2014 from the Amsterdam News, one of the nation’s oldest African American newspapers.

With an impressive Instagram following of 25K, Smalls has made notable appearances on national television to magazines and news programs, has appeared as a celebrity judge on “Top Chef” twice, and made guest appearances on “The Chew” with Carla Hall and “Extra Virgin” with Debi Mazar and Gabriele Corcos. He was recently featured in the Hallmark series “Meet the Peetes.”

RENAISSANCE MAN & EPIC DINNER PARTIES

His culinary journey has been chronicled on The Food Network, including shows like “Recipe for Success” and “Throwdown! with Bobby Flay,” as well as on NBC’s “The Today Show.”

Notably, Food & Wine spotlighted him on the cover of its October 2020 issue, highlighting his role as a prominent NYC host in his exquisite Harlem residence. Smalls was also a featured chef and author at the inaugural New York Times Food and Wine Festival in 2019.

Despite his numerous achievements, hosting dinner parties continues to be at the forefront for him. His passion for entertaining not only serves as a grounding force but also as a means to connect with fellow individuals engaged in compelling endeavors within the Black community.

His memoir and cookbook is titled “Grace the Table,” and showcases recipes deeply rooted in his upbringing with Southern Revival cuisine. In 2021, Smalls signed a deal for his inaugural children’s book, slated for publication by Denene Millner Books.

According to Eater, Smalls relocated to New York in the 1970s, and has he lived in several neighborhoods, including Soho, the Village, and Upper West Side. In each, he has hosted dinners and gatherings for his varied friend groups, one more eclectic than the next.

Harlem became his residence in 1998, a home he has fashioned into a museum of sorts, home to art collections by artists spanning the Black diaspora, from Rwanda to Brazil, and memorabilia from his experiences with Black Hollywood.
