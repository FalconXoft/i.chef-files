An Iconic Seafood Restaurant to Reopen After Nearly 20 Years

Plus, the last location of Boris & Horton is on the way out — and more intel

by Melissa McCart
Nov 11, 2024, 3:08 PM UTC
Staff photo by Derek Davis/Portland Press Herald via Getty Images
Melissa McCart is the lead editor of the Northeast region with more than 20 years of experience as a reporter, critic, editor, and cookbook author.

Lundy’s, which first opened in 1920, had been a seafood icon of Sheepshead Bay until it closed in 1979, then reopened and shuttered in 2007. Now there’s an attempt to open Lundy’s — at 44 Beard Street, at Dwight Street — in Red Hook, Brooklyn. The project is spearheaded by Sandra Snyder (wife of Red Hook Winery’s Mark Snyder); She told Gothamist that Frank Cretella, who revived Lundy’s in the ’90s (and owns the name), handed the name over and “wished us well,” she said. The Snyders say it’s a “nostalgic place” for them and want to bring back “old-school dining” to Brooklyn.

With seats for over 2,000 diners, the original restaurant was founded by Fredrick William Irving Lundy, who got his start by selling clams from a cart. While the restaurant was open, the family also owned Lundy Brothers fish market.

French wine bars are stronger than ever

Fort Greene’s Fradei Bistro is opening a sibling in Clinton Hill (39 Clifton Place at Lafayette Avenue) they’ve announced on Instagram. Entre Nous is a 50-seat wine bar “inspired by our favorite wine bars in Paris,” reads the job listing. It will serve small plates, chilled seafood, cheese, and charcuterie. The spot joins a growing collection of French wine bars across the city. Fradei first opened in 2018 starting as a tasting menu restaurant reviewed by the New York Times; it’s now a la carte.

A niche bakery in the East Village

Mary O’s Irish Soda Bread Shop opened over the weekend at 93 1/2 E. Seventh Street, at First Avenue. The shop is a sibling to the East Village Irish pub Mary O’s (32 Avenue A, at East Third Street) from Mary O’Halloran. She started making soda bread as a pandemic pop-up.

Boris & Horton’s last location is in trouble

Despite raising over $250,000 in crowdfunding to keep dog cafe Boris & Horton open, the team closed its Brooklyn location shortly after — a move that received some criticism. Now, its lone location in the East Village is also shutting down. On Instagram, the co-owners Logan Mikhly and Coppy Holzman are seeking a buyer. “Unless new ownership is found,” they wrote, “we will be closing our doors this month.” After the crowdfunding at the announcement of the Brooklyn closing, some donors questioned where the money went; the owners said it paid salaries, upgrades to an HVAC system, and refrigeration.
